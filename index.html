Discovery Call Framework
12 questions in order — copy to your notes before every call
Phase 1 — Understand the current state
01
Walk me through how your team handles [process] today, step by step.
02
How many people touch this process each week, and how much time does it take them?
03
What tools are you using right now — Excel, email, paper? What's broken about it?
04
What happens when something falls through the cracks? Can you give me a recent example?
Phase 2 — Quantify the pain
05
If I could eliminate this problem entirely, what would that free up for your team?
06
Has this caused a mistake that cost you money or a client? What was that worth?
07
Have you tried to fix this before internally? What happened?
Phase 3 — Qualify budget and decision
08
Is solving this a priority this quarter, or is it more of a nice-to-have?
09
Have you budgeted for this kind of project? Roughly what range were you thinking?
10
Who else needs to be involved in the decision — is it just you?
Phase 4 — Close the call
11
Based on what you've told me, I think I can build exactly what you need. Would it make sense for me to send you a proposal this week?
12
If the proposal looks good, what would next steps look like on your end?
Objection Responses
Click an objection to see what to say — word for word
"It's too expensive."
I understand — price is always a consideration. Let me ask: if this system saves your team 10 hours a week, what's that worth over a year? Most clients find the system pays for itself within 2–3 months. Would it help if I showed you how I arrived at this number?
"We'll build it internally."
Absolutely — and many companies try that. The challenge is that internal teams are usually pulled between this project and their actual job. I can deliver a working system in under two weeks. What's a realistic timeline if your team takes it on themselves?
"Not the right time."
I hear that a lot. Can I ask — what needs to happen for this to become a priority? If it's a Q3 budget issue, I can hold a proposal and follow up then. I'd rather work with your timeline than push for something that doesn't fit.
"Can you do it for cheaper?"
I can look at reducing scope — we could phase the project and start with the core module only. That said, I'd rather not cut corners on something your team will use every day. What part of the system is most critical to you right now?
"We already have software for that."
Good to know — what are you using? Generic software is built for everyone, which means it fits no one perfectly. What I build is designed around your exact workflow. Is your current tool covering everything, or are there still gaps?
"Send me some information."
Of course. Before I send anything — is there a specific concern I haven't addressed yet? I want to make sure what I send is useful, not generic. And when should I follow up after you've had a chance to read it?
